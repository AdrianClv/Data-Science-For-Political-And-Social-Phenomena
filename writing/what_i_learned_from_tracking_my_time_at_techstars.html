What I Learned Tracking My Time At Techstars

In the fall of 2015, Popily, a company I cofounded and work at as the head of product, was offered a slot in the 2016 class of Techstars Cloud. It was a great feeling and we were happy to accept, but in truth while I knew how accelerators like Techstars worked, I barely knew anything about the day to day life of someone in the program. How many hours a day do people work? Do they have time to code or is it guest speaker after guest speaker? I had watched Techstars video series following previous classes, but at the end of three seasons it seemed as if all the founders were able to work 36 hours every day while also having copious amount of leisure time riding bikes around Boulder. I also found some good posts about the day to day life, but these seemed to face other problems: people lie (to themselves and others), forget, and mis-remember. Furthermore, it is notably difficult to summarize 90+ days of your life in a short paragraph. So, seeing a gap in knowledge, when we started Techstars Cloud did what I was trained to do: gather data.

For 84 days, from November 2, 2015 to January 24, 2016 (the program actually ends February 11th) I recorded how I spent every 15 minutes of every day. Every 15 minute period was categorized as one of seven activity types:

  1. Non-technical Work - Any work on Popily that did not involve technical skills, including writing, emailing, diagramming, and managing product development.
  2. Technical Work - Any work involving coding, statistics, or other hards skills. Technical work typically included coding the product itself, doing analyses of user behavior, and designing the analytics backend.
  3. Discussion - Any work concerning Popily where the main activity was talking with people, including internal meetings with my cofounders, meetings with mentors and potential investors, Techstars guest speakers, etc. While it might seem odd to lump internal strategy meetings with Techstars events, I can say with total confidence that any discussion other than with family or friends over the during the 84 days concerned Popily.
  4. Sleep - Any time I was sleeping in a bed or (occasionally) in my car.
  5. Personal - Any time spent on non-Popily tasks like watching a movie or spending time with my family.
  6. Travel - Any time I was focused on traveling (notably driving) and not able to do any work.
  7. Exercise - Any time I was out on a run, which was essentially never.

The complete datasets and explanation of the data collection methodology is available on Github, however here I wanted to use the data explore how I spent my during Techstars and offer a few lessons learned from the experience.
